Problem 15 — 3.2 Exercises (Indeterminate Forms and L'Hôpital's Rule)

Evaluate \(\ds \lim_{x \to 0^+} \left(\csc x \right)^{x}\) or state that it does not exist.

SOLUTION The limit is in the indeterminate form \(\infty^0.\) We will let \(y = \left(\csc x \right)^{x}\) because it then follows that \[\ln(y) = x \ln(\csc x),\] and considering \(\ds \lim_{x \to 0^+} \ln(y)\) yields the indeterminate form \(0 \times \infty.\) However, \(\ds \lim_{x \to 0^+} \ln(y)\) can be rewritten as \[\lim_{x \to 0^+} \frac{\ln(\csc x)}{1/x},\] which is in the indeterminate form \(\infty/\infty.\) Therefore, applying L'Hôpital's Rule gives \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 0^+} \ln(y) &= \lim_{x \to 0^+} \frac{\frac{\dd}{\dd x} \ln(\csc x)}{\frac{\dd}{\dd x} 1/x} \\ &= \lim_{x \to 0^+} \frac{-\csc(x)\cot(x)/\csc(x)}{-1/x^2} \\ &= \lim_{x \to 0^+} x^2 \cot(x) \\ &= \lim_{x \to 0^+} \frac{x^2 \cos(x)}{\sin(x)}, \\ \ea \] which is in the indeterminate form \(0/0.\) Thus, applying L'Hôpital's Rule again produces \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 0^+} \ln(y) &= \lim_{x \to 0^+} \frac{\frac{\dd}{\dd x} x^2 \cos(x)}{\frac{\dd}{\dd x} \sin(x)} \\ &= \lim_{x \to 0^+} \frac{2x\cos(x) - x^2 \sin(x)}{\cos(x)} \\ &= 0. \ea \] Because \(\ds \lim_{x \to 0^+} \ln(y)= 0,\) it follows that \(\ds \lim_{x \to 0^+} y = e^0 = \box{1.}\)
