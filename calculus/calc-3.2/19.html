Problem 19 — 3.2 Exercises (Indeterminate Forms and L'Hôpital's Rule)

Let \(p\) and \(q\) be differentiable functions. Let \(q\) be a function defined by \(\ds q(x) = \frac{2p(x)^3 + 4}{x^2 - 6x}\) that satisfies \(\ds \lim_{x \to 0} q(x) = 12.\) It is known that \(\ds \lim_{x \to 0} q(x)\) can be evaluated using L'Hôpital's Rule. Find \(p(0)\) and \(p'(0).\)

SOLUTION In order to apply L'Hôpital's Rule, \(\ds \lim_{x \to 0} q(x)\) must either be in the indeterminate form \(0/0\) or \(\infty/\infty.\) \(p\) is differentiable and therefore continuous, so it follows that \(\ds \lim_{x \to 0} p(x) = p(0).\) Thus, \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 0} 2p(x)^3 + 4 &= \lim_{x \to 0} (x^2 - 6x) = 0 \\ 2p(0)^3 + 4 &= 0 \\ p(0) &= -\sqrt[3]{2}. \\ \ea \] Applying L'Hôpital's Rule, we get \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 0} q(x) = \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\frac{\dd}{\dd x} \left(2 p(x)^3 + 4\right)}{\frac{\dd}{\dd x}(x^2 - 6x)} &= 12 \\ \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{6p'(x)}{2x - 6} &= 12 \\ \frac{6p'(0)}{-6} &= 12 \\ p'(0) &= -12. \ea \] Summarizing, \[ \box{ \ba p(0) &= -\sqrt[3]{2}. \\ p'(0) &= -12. \ea } \]
